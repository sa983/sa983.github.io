About

Hi! I am Saatvik, a senior at Cornell studying Industrial & Labor Relations with a concentration in Public Policy. At Cornell, I am a Rawlings Presidential Research Scholar which generously funds my research assistantship with the National Zoning Atlas (formerly housed in the Legal Constructs Lab @ Cornell AAP). My current research work focuses on understanding how zoning affects what can be built, where it can be built, and how it shapes inequality. I am currently co-authoring a paper on how zoning was used by localities to preserve racial affluence.

My scholarly interests span the social sciences with particular interests in housing policy, the social safety net, physical infrastructure, and state capacity. Personally, you can find me cycling, enjoying California's protected lands, and consuming wonky podcasts.

Measuring Residential Segregation in Upstate New York

R • ggplot2 • sf • tidycensus

This scatter plot shows the relationship between racial segregation (measured by dissimilarity index) and median household income across census tracts in Buffalo and Rochester. Each point represents a census tract, demonstrating how areas with higher racial segregation tend to correlate with lower household incomes.

As part of the final project for ECON 3465, I explored different ways to measure residential segregation and its effects in Upstate New York. I used data from the American Community Survey along with two different techniques, dissimilarity indices and entropy indices (Theil's H), to map racial segregation in two Upstate cities: Buffalo and Rochester. I also regressed these measures with income, highlighting the correlation between segregation and urban poverty. My work provided background for our group's proposed evaluation of New York's Making Moves Program, a housing choice voucher system targeting racial imbalances.

Job Matching Field Experiment

Qualtrics • Prolific • tidyverse

Summary visualization of field experiment results showing the effect of job description length on application rates. The chart displays mean application rates across different treatment groups, with confidence intervals indicating statistical significance of the observed differences.

I independently designed and ran this field experiment as my final project for ECON 3488. This field experiment tested the effect that the length of job descriptions had on a candidate's propensity to apply. This experiment is based on prior research that has established how the design of job applications can perpetuate applicant gender imbalance along with growing media attention over high applicant attrition rates. As part of this project, I recruited participants, randomized them, collected data through Qualtrics, analyzed the data using R, and followed open source research practices throughout. The plots above show results from the experiment and a logit regression which breaks down the impact of candidate characteristics on decision making.
